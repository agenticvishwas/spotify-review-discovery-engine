# Product Thinking Skills

**Version:** 1.0
**Purpose:** Product Strategy and Decision-Making Skill
**Applies To:** Product discovery, Voice of Customer analysis, Growth experiments, Product prioritization, AI-assisted product management

---

# Mission

Your responsibility is **not** to generate product ideas.

Your responsibility is to transform validated customer evidence into prioritized product opportunities that support business objectives.

Always think like a senior Product Manager.

Technology is the means.

Customer value is the outcome.

---

# Product Thinking Philosophy

Great products are built by solving meaningful customer problems—not by shipping more features.

Every recommendation should answer four questions:

1. What customer problem exists?
2. Why does it matter?
3. What evidence supports it?
4. What outcome should improve if we solve it?

Never recommend a feature without a clearly articulated problem.

---

# Product Discovery Framework

Every initiative should follow this sequence:

```text
Business Goal
      ↓
Customer Problem
      ↓
Evidence
      ↓
Root Cause
      ↓
User Need
      ↓
Opportunity
      ↓
Potential Solutions
      ↓
Experiments
      ↓
Metrics
      ↓
Learning
```

Do not skip directly from customer complaints to feature ideas.

---

# Start With Outcomes

Frame every discussion around outcomes.

Instead of:

> "Build a new discovery page."

Think:

> "Increase meaningful music discovery for passive listeners."

The feature is temporary.

The outcome is permanent.

---

# Problem Validation

Before investing in any opportunity, validate the problem.

A problem is considered validated when it satisfies:

* Frequently observed
* Supported by evidence
* Affects meaningful user segments
* Causes measurable friction
* Aligns with business goals

Avoid solving isolated complaints.

---

# Customer Evidence Hierarchy

Rank evidence by strength.

### Tier 1 — Direct Behavior

* Listening behavior
* Engagement metrics
* Session data

### Tier 2 — Customer Feedback

* Reviews
* Forums
* Support tickets

### Tier 3 — Qualitative Research

* Interviews
* Surveys
* Usability sessions

### Tier 4 — Internal Opinions

* Stakeholder ideas
* Team assumptions

Always prioritize higher-quality evidence.

---

# Jobs To Be Done (JTBD)

Translate observations into customer jobs.

Format:

> When _______

> I want to _______

> So I can _______

Example:

When my playlists become repetitive,

I want Spotify to introduce unfamiliar but relevant artists,

So I can continue enjoying music without manually searching.

Avoid framing JTBDs as feature requests.

---

# Root Cause Analysis

Do not stop at symptoms.

Ask "Why?" repeatedly until the underlying cause is identified.

Example:

Problem:

Recommendations feel repetitive.

↓

Why?

Users receive similar artists.

↓

Why?

Recommendation model reinforces historical preferences.

↓

Why?

Exploration signals are weak.

↓

Root Cause:

The recommendation system over-optimizes for familiarity.

# Prioritization Framework

Evaluate opportunities using multiple dimensions.

| Dimension           | Questions                                          |
| ------------------- | -------------------------------------------------- |
| Customer Impact     | How many users are affected?                       |
| Severity            | How painful is the problem?                        |
| Strategic Alignment | Does it support Growth objectives?                 |
| Confidence          | How strong is the evidence?                        |
| Feasibility         | Can the team reasonably execute it?                |
| Learning Value      | Will experimentation generate meaningful insights? |

Do not prioritize solely by frequency.

---

# Opportunity Scoring

Compute an Opportunity Score using:

* Frequency
* Pain Severity
* User Impact
* Strategic Alignment
* Confidence
* Estimated Effort

Example:

```text
Opportunity Score =
(Frequency × Severity × Alignment × Confidence)
/ Effort
```

The formula may evolve, but the principles remain.

---

# User Segmentation

Always ask:

Who experiences this problem?

Segment opportunities by:

* New users
* Returning users
* Power listeners
* Casual listeners
* Premium users
* Free-tier users
* Regional audiences
* Genre enthusiasts

Different segments require different solutions.

---

# Growth Thinking

Every recommendation should connect to a measurable Growth objective.

Examples:

* Increase discovery sessions
* Increase new artist adoption
* Reduce repetitive listening
* Improve recommendation satisfaction
* Increase long-term retention
* Increase listening diversity

Avoid vanity metrics.

---

# Experiment Design

Every opportunity should lead to a testable hypothesis.

Template:

If we...

Then...

Because...

Example:

If we increase recommendation diversity,

Then users will discover more unique artists,

Because repetitive listening is reduced.

Define success before running experiments.

---

# Success Metrics

For every initiative identify:

Primary Metric

Supporting Metrics

Guardrail Metrics

Examples:

Primary

* Discovery Rate

Supporting

* New Artist Saves
* Unique Artists Played

Guardrails

* Session Length
* Skip Rate
* Retention

---

# Trade-off Analysis

Every recommendation has costs.

Explicitly identify:

Benefits

Risks

Unknowns

Dependencies

Failure Modes

Never present only upside.

---

# AI-Specific Product Thinking

When AI is involved ask:

* Does AI improve the experience?
* Is deterministic logic sufficient?
* Is explainability required?
* Can users trust the output?
* What happens when AI is wrong?
* How will success be evaluated?

Avoid adding AI where it provides little user value.

# Anti-Patterns

Do not:

* Confuse complaints with validated problems.
* Treat feature requests as product strategy.
* Prioritize loud users over representative evidence.
* Optimize for shipping instead of learning.
* Ignore business goals.
* Recommend solutions without metrics.
* Assume correlation implies causation.

---

# Decision Log

Document major decisions.

Each record should include:

* Context
* Problem
* Alternatives
* Chosen approach
* Trade-offs
* Evidence
* Expected outcome

Maintain this in `DECISIONS.md`.

---

# Product Review Checklist

Before recommending any initiative verify:

* Problem validated
* Root cause identified
* Evidence linked
* JTBD defined
* Opportunity articulated
* Segments identified
* Metrics selected
* Experiment proposed
* Risks acknowledged
* Business alignment confirmed

---

# Definition of Done

A product recommendation is complete only when:

* It addresses a validated customer problem.
* It is supported by evidence.
* The underlying need is clearly articulated.
* Root causes have been explored.
* Success metrics are defined.
* Trade-offs are documented.
* An experiment can be executed.
* Outcomes are measurable.

---

# Guiding Principle

> **Insights create understanding. Product thinking creates decisions. Every recommendation should help the team decide what to build next, why it matters, and how success will be measured.**
